## 3) Any issues that may require future legislation
Even without testimony, HB 1532 itself **creates multiple future decision points and study/proceeding obligations** that may require either:
- implementing regulations/orders by agencies, or
- follow-on legislation depending on outcomes.

Key “future legislation / follow-on action” triggers in the bill include:

1. **Successor to net energy metering (NEM)**
   - PSC must run a proceeding and issue recommendations by **Dec. 15, 2026**, including **any legislative changes necessary**.
   - Implementing statute is already provided (successor program framework), but further statutory tweaks may be needed depending on PSC recommendations.

2. **Third-party single administrator for utility energy programs**
   - PSC issues an RFI (due **July 1, 2027**) and then an RFP and selects an administrator by **June 1, 2028** for administration beginning the **3-year cycle starting 2030**.
   - If PSC concludes structural changes are needed, future legislation could be requested.

3. **Transmission congestion reporting / advanced transmission technology reporting**
   - Owners/operators must submit recurring reports (every 4 years).
   - The bill builds obligations that could later require legislative clarification if technical standards or funding mechanisms need statute.

4. **Large load customer rate schedule regulations**
   - PSC adoption of regulations by **June 1, 2026**.

5. **Electric universal service program transfer to Office of Home Energy Programs**
   - Requires operational realignment; additional legislative adjustments might be proposed if program administration or eligibility/benefit sizing becomes contentious.

6. **Power Plant Research Program permitting streamlining study**
   - Report by **Dec. 31, 2026**; may lead to statutory permitting/zoning changes to implement “fast-tracked development.”

7. **Solar permitting software and permitting fee constraints**
   - Deadlines include **Aug. 1, 2027** (fee cap eligibility) and **July 1, 2028** (remote inspection option if inspection times exceed threshold).
   - Implementation may require statutory refinement if local compliance/administrative feasibility becomes an issue.

8. **Alternative compliance fee auctions**
   - Administration/PSC set capacity targets and conduct annual auctions for **2027 and 2028**.
   - If procurement rules or incentive eligibility need tightening, future legislation may follow.

9. **Nuclear long-term pricing / zero-emission credit definitions and escrow mechanics**
   - Requires regulatory rulemaking, escrow setup, and potentially later adjustments to rate impact modeling and credit valuation.

## 4) Any questions raised
**No hearing transcript was provided and no testimony content is included**, so I cannot attribute “questions raised” to particular witnesses.

However, HB 1532 contains provisions that commonly generate committee questions and may be worth proactively preparing for:

1. **Electric universal service program transfer**
   - How will oversight work in practice once the program is housed in the Office of Home Energy Programs (and what happens to administrative roles)?
   - How will the “$37 million” cap and allocation mechanics interact with actual need over time?

2. **Large load customer threshold changes**
   - The bill shifts the “large load customer” definition (e.g., **100 MW → 25 MW** changes and load factor adjustments).
   - Likely questions: scope expansion magnitude; competitive impacts; customer subsidy protection.

3. **Energy efficiency / conservation greenhouse-gas accounting changes**
   - The bill changes target calculations (behind-the-meter requirements; community solar caps for 2027–2029; changes to weights).
   - Questions likely: what counts toward targets; how “equity” and “ratepayer impacts” are measured; interactions with electric customer choice.

4. **Net metering successor program**
   - How will “fair compensation” vs. ratepayer cost minimization be balanced?
   - How are non-participants’ equity impacts quantified?

5. **Solar permitting software requirements**
   - Feasibility and funding adequacy for local governments.
   - Enforcement and whether fee caps and remote inspection requirements are operationally workable.

6. **Alternative compliance fee auctions**
   - How will “low-bid” auctions be designed to avoid delivering stranded or underperforming capacity?
   - Bonding, milestones, and recapture standards.

7. **Transmission line siting/permit process changes**
   - HB text includes numerous notice/hearing requirements and constraints tied to certificate of public convenience and necessity.
   - Questions likely: local authority boundaries; preemption; timeline impacts.
